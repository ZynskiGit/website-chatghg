SqFt vs Emissions

From this graph, we can observe how year over year since 2005, Northeastern’s campus has only grown in percentage of square footage. In contrast, we can see that year over year, Northeastern’s emissions have decreased more than they’ve increased in percentage. Typically, you would expect a campus to increase emissions as the amount of land and buildings increases. Explanations for this include the replacement of natural gas over oil and newer or more renovated buildings that work more efficiently.

Consumption Per Energy Source.

This visualization illustrates the trending of Northeastern University’s energy sources over the last 16 years, as measured in mmBTUs (Metric Million British Thermal Unit,).  Oil use has been trending down significantly, while electricity usage has been trending up, taking a marked jump in 2014. This a good thing, as the University has taken great strides in electrifying campus buildings. Surprisingly, while natural gas use has risen, it is not by much. After 2015, there seems to be some relationship between gas and electricity sources, as they rise and fall in similar amounts.  If we are to meet the City of Boston’s BERDO ordinance, natural gas would need to trend down while electricity usage continues to rise.

Disposed consistently holds the majority percentage from 2019, indicating that more than half of waste is not recycled, composted, or donated/sold. While on the other hand, the combination of composing and recycling is consistently growing across the years despite the dip in the single recycling category, indicating a growing efforts in these categories. Waste Type by Year

This plot shows the disposition of waste materials at Northeastern university over the past 4 years, broken down by the material types. We can see that proportionally, Trash disposal has continued to be the leading overall disposition method. Composting has taken a marked jump forward. After taking a dip in 2020, it has grown as a disposition method in both 2021 and 2022. Other/Mixed Recycling has grown the most within the Recycling category.

Waste Management Disposition

NU Waste and Recycling Method Over Time

This plot shows the disposition of waste materials at Northeastern university over the past 4 years. Each point represents a waste material type (waste, cardboard, compost, textile, etc.).   I was surprised to fin that although waste/trash disposition initially decreased in 2020, it has been trending upwards over the past few years. Similarly, recycling and composting  both took a significant dip in 2020 (recycling more than composting), and have been slowly edging back up, but are still yet to overtake the waste/trash as the highest disposition method.  I believe it is likely that these two more sustainable disposition methods have seen growth due to various initiatives that the Northeastern’s Climate Justice & Sustainability Hub has put in place, like composting programs at the dining areas and dorms and increased community awareness.
