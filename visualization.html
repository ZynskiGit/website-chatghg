Explore our visual progress in sustainability through key metrics. From waste reduction to energy diversification, our charts and graphs offer a snapshot of the impact we're making. Each visualization captures a piece of our journey towards a greener, more equitable campus life.

How are we managing waste?

Waste Management Trends at Northeastern

Our data reveals an intriguing trend in waste management from the last four years. Despite a notable reduction in general waste in 2020, this category has seen a gradual increase since. Meanwhile, recycling and composting experienced a decline during the same period, with a slow yet steady recovery thereafter. This shift likely reflects the positive impact of Northeastern’s Climate Justice & Sustainability Hub's initiatives, such as enhanced composting facilities and heightened environmental consciousness within our community.

NU Waste Management Disposition Over Time
Glickel, J. (2023). Waste & Recycling Summary. Boston; Northeastern University’s Climate Justice & Sustainability Hub.

Data from 2019 onwards shows that the majority of waste hasn't been recycled or composted, highlighting a challenge in waste reduction. Despite this, there's a positive trend in combined recycling and composting efforts, suggesting a commitment to sustainability. However, the minimal waste diverted through donations or sales points to potential gaps in supporting policies or infrastructure.

What about Food Waste?

As we strive for a more sustainable future, understanding the patterns of food waste within our community is crucial. Our latest analysis focuses on various locations across Northeastern, offering insightful revelations into how and where food waste is most prominently generated.

Food Service History,
Glickel, J. (2023). NU_Service_History. Boston; Northeastern University’s Dining Services Dept

Examining the food waste patterns at Northeastern reveals anticipated trends and raises fresh inquiries. Predictably, the IV and Stetson West loading docks, associated with dining services, are the primary contributors to food waste—their median values surpass even the upper extremes of other locations. The notable spread of outlier data at Speare could be attributed to variable events. Interestingly, Curry ranks third in food waste generation, lagging noticeably behind IV and Stetson West.

GHG Emission

Annual emissions are quantified in terms of carbon dioxide equivalents (CO2e), presented in both kilograms and metric tons. These statistics represent the main campus's output and exclude emissions from Northeastern University's additional campuses outside Massachusetts.

Campus GHG Emissions,
Glickel, J. (2023). NU MA Campus GHG Inventory 2005-2022. Boston; Northeastern University’s Climate Justice & Sustainability Hub

SqFt vs Emissions

This graph depicts a remarkable trend: even as Northeastern’s campus square footage has expanded consistently since 2005, the university’s emissions have not followed the expected upward trajectory. Instead, they have been generally decreasing. The y-axis measures the percentage change from the previous year, highlighting this positive shift. Factors contributing to this encouraging environmental impact include transitioning from oil to natural gas and the construction of newer, more energy-efficient buildings.

Building CO2 Emissions

Is Northeastern on track with the BERDO2030 benchmarks for building emissions? An examination of the graph, where the darker green bars represent the BERDO2030 goals and the lighter green bars indicate current progress, reveals the university's position in reducing CO2 emissions. Notably, the 'Egan Engineering Science Research Center' stands out with the highest emission rates. However, a detailed analysis shows that approximately 27% of the buildings are already meeting the BERDO2030 standards, illustrating a commitment to sustainability and a push for further improvements.

Where and how are energy being used?

Total Northeastern University Energy Consumption Over Time ,
Glickel, J. (2023). NU MA Campus - Energy Inventory -2005-2022. Boston; Northeastern University’s Climate Justice & Sustainability Hub
University Energy Consumption Over Time by Type,
Glickel, J. (2023). NU MA Campus - Energy Inventory -2005-2022. Boston; Northeastern University’s Climate Justice & Sustainability Hub

The pair of charts offer a comprehensive look into Northeastern University's energy narrative. The left chart maps out the total energy consumption of the university, while the right chart dissects the specific energy sources over the past 16 years, both measured in mmBTUs. A significant decline in oil use contrasts with the surge in electricity, with a notable increase in 2014, mirroring the university's proactive transition to electrifying its infrastructure. Although there's been a slight uptick in natural gas usage, it pales in comparison to the rise in electricity, with post-2015 trends suggesting a correlation between the two. To adhere to Boston's BERDO ordinance, a sustained decrease in natural gas coupled with an increased reliance on electricity will be crucial.
